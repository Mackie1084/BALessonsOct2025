üîê Internal Controls

What are Internal Controls?

Processes and procedures to ensure:

  • Reliable financial reporting
  • Compliance with laws and regulations
  • Effective operations
  • Protection of assets
  • Prevention and detection of fraud

Why Controls Matter in Singapore

  • Required by Companies Act
  • ACRA expects proper controls
  • Auditors assess effectiveness
  • Protects company reputation
  • Reduces fraud risk

üéØ Key Control Activities

Segregation of Duties

Different people handle different aspects

Example:

  • Person A: Orders goods
  • Person B: Receives goods
  • Person C: Approves payment
  • Person D: Issues payment

Authorization

Proper approval required

Example:

  • Under $1,000: Supervisor
  • $1,000-$10,000: Manager
  • Over $10,000: Director

Physical Controls

  • Locked cash boxes
  • Secured inventory
  • CCTV surveillance
  • Access controls

Reconciliations

  • Bank reconciliations
  • Inventory counts
  • Petty cash verification
  • Regular reviews

üí∞ Petty Cash System

What is Petty Cash?

Small amount of cash kept on hand for minor expenses

Typical Uses

  • Office supplies
  • Postage fees
  • Staff refreshments
  • Taxi fares
  • Parking fees
  • Emergency purchases

Singapore Context

Typical limits: SGD $500 - $2,000

Transaction limit: SGD $50 - $100

GST: Can be claimed with proper receipts

üîÑ Imprest System

What is the Imprest System?

Petty cash maintained at a fixed amount. When spent, it is replenished back to the original amount.

Example: How it Works

Start with petty cash SGD 500
Payments made during period SGD 280
Cash remaining SGD 220
Vouchers total SGD 280
Cash + Vouchers = Imprest SGD 500
Replenishment from bank SGD 280
New petty cash balance SGD 500

Formula: Cash on Hand + Vouchers = Imprest Amount

üè¶ Bank Reconciliation

What is Bank Reconciliation?

Comparing the company's Cash Book with the Bank Statement to identify and explain differences.

Why Reconcile?

  • Verify accuracy
  • Detect errors
  • Identify unauthorized transactions
  • Discover bank charges
  • Detect fraud

When to Reconcile?

Minimum: Monthly

Best practice: Weekly

Large companies: Daily

Singapore Banking

Major banks: DBS, OCBC, UOB

Payment methods: PayNow, FAST, GIRO

Online banking: Real-time statements available

üîç Why Cash Book ‚â† Bank Statement?

1. Outstanding Cheques

Company issued cheque but recipient hasn't deposited yet

Effect: Cash Book shows LESS than Bank Statement

2. Deposits in Transit

Company deposited money but bank hasn't processed yet

Effect: Cash Book shows MORE than Bank Statement

3. Items on Bank Statement Not in Cash Book

  • Bank charges and fees
  • Interest earned
  • Direct debits (GIRO payments)
  • Direct credits (customer PayNow payments)
  • NSF (bounced) cheques

4. Errors

  • Company errors (wrong amounts recorded)
  • Bank errors (very rare)

üí∏ Cash Flow Statement

What is a Cash Flow Statement?

Shows inflows and outflows of cash during a period, classified into three categories.

Why It Matters

  • Shows actual cash generation
  • Reveals liquidity position
  • Different from profit
  • Assesses ability to pay debts
  • Required by SFRS

Key Concept

PROFIT ‚â† CASH

A company can be profitable but run out of cash!

üìÇ Three Categories of Cash Flows

1. Operating Activities

Cash from day-to-day operations

Inflows:

  • Cash from customers
  • Interest received
  • Dividends received

Outflows:

  • Cash to suppliers
  • Salaries paid
  • Rent, utilities
  • Interest paid
  • Tax paid

2. Investing Activities

Cash from buying/selling long-term assets

Inflows:

  • Sale of equipment
  • Sale of property
  • Sale of investments

Outflows:

  • Purchase equipment
  • Purchase property
  • Purchase investments

3. Financing Activities

Cash from/to owners and creditors

Inflows:

  • Owner contributions
  • Issue shares
  • Borrowing loans

Outflows:

  • Owner drawings
  • Dividends paid
  • Loan repayments

üíµ Free Cash Flow

What is Free Cash Flow?

Cash available after maintaining or expanding the asset base.

Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures

TechVenture Example

Operating Cash Flow SGD 2,000
Less: Capital Expenditures (SGD 70,000)
Free Cash Flow (SGD 68,000)

‚ö†Ô∏è Negative - needs external financing

Positive FCF

  • Can pay dividends
  • Can reduce debt
  • Financial flexibility
  • Less external financing

Negative FCF

May be OK if:

  • Start-up phase
  • Growth investments
  • Strategic expansion

Concern if:

  • Consistently negative
  • Operating cash negative

‚úÖ Best Practices Summary

Internal Controls

  • Segregate duties properly
  • Require authorization for payments
  • Physical security for assets
  • Regular reconciliations
  • Monitor and review

Petty Cash

  • Use imprest system
  • One designated custodian
  • Pre-numbered vouchers
  • Require receipts
  • Regular surprise counts

Bank Reconciliation

  • Reconcile at least monthly
  • Independent person performs it
  • Record adjusting entries promptly
  • Investigate all differences
  • Document the process

Cash Flow Management

  • Prepare cash flow forecasts
  • Monitor operating cash flow
  • Speed up collections
  • Optimize payment timing
  • Use technology (online banking)
