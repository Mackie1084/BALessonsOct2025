1. Accrued Expense

An accrued expense is an expense that has been incurred but not yet paid. It represents a liability that a company owes.

Key Characteristics:

  • Expense has been incurred (service received or goods consumed)
  • Payment has not yet been made
  • Recorded to match expenses with the period they relate to
Example: A company receives electricity service in December worth $500 but receives the bill in January.

Journal Entry (December):

Dr. Electricity Expense      $500
    Cr. Accrued Expenses          $500
Remember: Accrued expenses ensure expenses are recorded in the correct accounting period, following the matching principle.

2. Prepaid Expense

A prepaid expense is a payment made in advance for goods or services to be received in the future. It's recorded as an asset.

Key Characteristics:

  • Payment made before receiving the benefit
  • Initially recorded as an asset
  • Gradually expensed as the benefit is consumed
Example: A company pays $1,200 for annual insurance on January 1st.

Initial Journal Entry (January 1):

Dr. Prepaid Insurance      $1,200
    Cr. Cash/Bank                 $1,200

Monthly Adjustment (End of each month):

Dr. Insurance Expense      $100
    Cr. Prepaid Insurance        $100

3. Accrued Revenue

Accrued revenue is revenue that has been earned but not yet received in cash. It represents an asset (accounts receivable).

Key Characteristics:

  • Revenue has been earned (service provided or goods delivered)
  • Payment has not yet been received
  • Recorded to recognize revenue in the correct period
Example: A consulting firm completes $3,000 worth of work in December but won't invoice until January.

Journal Entry (December):

Dr. Accrued Revenue/Accounts Receivable    $3,000
    Cr. Service Revenue                         $3,000
Remember: Accrued revenue follows the revenue recognition principle - revenue is recognized when earned, not when cash is received.

4. Advanced Revenue (Unearned Revenue)

Advanced revenue (also called deferred revenue or unearned revenue) is payment received before goods are delivered or services are performed. It's a liability.

Key Characteristics:

  • Cash received in advance
  • Obligation to deliver goods/services in the future
  • Initially recorded as a liability
  • Recognized as revenue when earned
Example: A magazine company receives $600 for a 1-year subscription on July 1st.

Initial Journal Entry (July 1):

Dr. Cash/Bank                 $600
    Cr. Unearned Revenue      $600

Monthly Adjustment (End of each month):

Dr. Unearned Revenue      $50
    Cr. Subscription Revenue    $50

5. Provision for Doubtful Debts

Provision for doubtful debts is an estimate of accounts receivable that may not be collected. It's a contra-asset account.

Purpose:

  • Anticipate potential losses from uncollectible accounts
  • Present accounts receivable at net realizable value
  • Follow the prudence/conservatism principle
Example: A company has $50,000 in accounts receivable and estimates 5% may be uncollectible.

Journal Entry:

Dr. Bad Debt Expense             $2,500
    Cr. Provision for Doubtful Debts    $2,500

Balance Sheet Presentation:

Accounts Receivable $50,000
Less: Provision for Doubtful Debts ($2,500)
Net Accounts Receivable $47,500

6. Bad Debts – Direct Write-Off Method

The direct write-off method records bad debts only when specific accounts are determined to be uncollectible.

Characteristics:

  • Bad debt expense recorded when account is deemed uncollectible
  • No provision account used
  • Simple but may violate matching principle
  • Often used by small businesses
Example: Customer ABC owes $1,500 and declares bankruptcy. The company decides to write off the debt.

Journal Entry:

Dr. Bad Debt Expense        $1,500
    Cr. Accounts Receivable      $1,500

If Later Recovered:

Dr. Accounts Receivable      $1,500
    Cr. Bad Debt Expense        $1,500

Dr. Cash/Bank               $1,500
    Cr. Accounts Receivable      $1,500

7. Bad Debts – Provision Method (Allowance Method)

The provision method estimates bad debts in advance and creates an allowance account.

Two-Step Process:

Step 1: Create/Adjust Provision (End of Period)

Dr. Bad Debt Expense             $2,000
    Cr. Provision for Doubtful Debts    $2,000

Step 2: Write Off Specific Account (When Identified)

Dr. Provision for Doubtful Debts    $500
    Cr. Accounts Receivable          $500
Key Difference: Writing off under the provision method does NOT create an expense. The expense was already recorded when the provision was created.

If Written-Off Debt is Later Recovered:

Dr. Accounts Receivable          $500
    Cr. Provision for Doubtful Debts    $500

Dr. Cash/Bank                    $500
    Cr. Accounts Receivable          $500

8. Adjusted Trial Balance

An adjusted trial balance is prepared after all adjusting entries have been posted. It serves as the basis for preparing financial statements.

Purpose:

  • Verify that total debits equal total credits after adjustments
  • Provide updated account balances
  • Serve as the foundation for financial statement preparation

Process:

1. Prepare unadjusted trial balance
2. Record adjusting entries (accruals, deferrals, estimates)
3. Update account balances
4. Prepare adjusted trial balance
5. Use adjusted trial balance to prepare financial statements

Common Adjusting Entries:

  • Accrued revenues and expenses
  • Prepaid expenses and unearned revenues
  • Depreciation
  • Provision for doubtful debts
  • Inventory adjustments
Remember: The adjusted trial balance must balance (total debits = total credits) before proceeding to financial statement preparation.

9. Standing Journals

Standing journals (recurring journals) are standard journal entries that are recorded regularly for routine transactions.

Characteristics:

  • Used for repetitive, predictable transactions
  • Pre-set templates that repeat monthly, quarterly, or annually
  • Improve efficiency and reduce errors
  • Can be automated in accounting systems

Common Examples:

1. Monthly Rent Expense
Dr. Rent Expense      $5,000
    Cr. Cash/Bank          $5,000
2. Monthly Depreciation
Dr. Depreciation Expense         $1,000
    Cr. Accumulated Depreciation    $1,000
3. Monthly Loan Interest
Dr. Interest Expense      $300
    Cr. Interest Payable    $300
Benefit: Standing journals streamline the accounting process and ensure consistency in recording routine transactions.

10. Reversing Entries

Reversing entries are journal entries made at the beginning of an accounting period to reverse certain adjusting entries from the previous period.

Purpose:

  • Simplify the recording of subsequent transactions
  • Prevent double-counting of revenues or expenses
  • Allow normal recording procedures in the new period

Which Adjusting Entries Should Be Reversed?

  • ✓ Accrued revenues
  • ✓ Accrued expenses
  • ✓ Prepaid expenses (if initially recorded as expense)
  • ✓ Unearned revenues (if initially recorded as revenue)
  • ✗ Depreciation
  • ✗ Provision for doubtful debts
Example: In December, a company accrued $500 for salaries.

December 31 (Adjusting Entry):
Dr. Salaries Expense    $500
    Cr. Salaries Payable    $500

January 1 (Reversing Entry):
Dr. Salaries Payable    $500
    Cr. Salaries Expense    $500

When Paid in January:
Dr. Salaries Expense    $800 (full payment)
    Cr. Cash/Bank           $800

Net effect: $300 expense for January ($800 - $500 reversal)

Summary: Key Accounting Concepts

Timing Adjustments:

  • Accrued Expense: Expense incurred, not yet paid (liability)
  • Prepaid Expense: Paid in advance, not yet incurred (asset)
  • Accrued Revenue: Revenue earned, not yet received (asset)
  • Advanced Revenue: Received in advance, not yet earned (liability)

Accounts Receivable Management:

  • Provision for Doubtful Debts: Estimated uncollectible amounts
  • Direct Method: Write off when identified as uncollectible
  • Provision Method: Estimate in advance, write off against provision

Process & Efficiency:

  • Adjusted Trial Balance: Updated balances after all adjustments
  • Standing Journals: Recurring entries for routine transactions
  • Reversing Entries: Simplify recording in the new period
Master these concepts to ensure accurate financial reporting and maintain proper accounting records!
