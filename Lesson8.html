üîí Closing General Ledger Accounts

What is the Closing Process?

The closing process is a series of journal entries made at the end of an accounting period to transfer balances from temporary accounts to permanent accounts.

Purpose of Closing Entries:
  • Reset temporary accounts to zero for the next accounting period
  • Transfer net income (or loss) to owner's equity
  • Update retained earnings for the period's results
  • Prepare accounts for the new accounting period
  • Separate each period's revenues and expenses

Temporary vs. Permanent Accounts

‚è≥ Temporary Accounts (Closed Annually)

Closed at period-end to zero balance

  • Revenue Accounts: Sales, Service Revenue, Interest Income
  • Expense Accounts: Rent, Salaries, Utilities, Depreciation
  • Dividends/Drawings: Distributions to owners
  • Income Summary: Temporary clearing account

‚Üí Balances go to ZERO after closing

‚ôæÔ∏è Permanent Accounts (Never Closed)

Carry balances forward to next period

  • Asset Accounts: Cash, Accounts Receivable, Equipment
  • Liability Accounts: Accounts Payable, Notes Payable
  • Equity Accounts: Capital, Retained Earnings

‚Üí Balances CONTINUE to next period

The Four Closing Entries

Entry 1: Close Revenue Accounts to Income Summary

DEBIT:
Sales Revenue $100,000
Service Revenue $25,000
Interest Revenue $1,000
CREDIT:
Income Summary $126,000

Purpose: Transfer all revenue balances to Income Summary

Entry 2: Close Expense Accounts to Income Summary

DEBIT:
Income Summary $90,000
CREDIT:
Cost of Goods Sold $50,000
Salaries Expense $25,000
Rent Expense $10,000
Utilities Expense $3,000
Depreciation Expense $2,000

Purpose: Transfer all expense balances to Income Summary

Income Summary Balance After Entries 1 & 2:
Credit (revenues): $126,000
Debit (expenses): $90,000
Balance = $36,000 CREDIT (Net Income)

Entry 3: Close Income Summary to Retained Earnings

DEBIT:
Income Summary $36,000
CREDIT:
Retained Earnings $36,000

Purpose: Transfer net income to equity (if net loss, reverse the entry)

Entry 4: Close Dividends/Drawings to Retained Earnings

DEBIT:
Retained Earnings $10,000
CREDIT:
Dividends $10,000

Purpose: Transfer dividends paid to equity (reduces retained earnings)

After Closing: Post-Closing Trial Balance

‚úÖ What Remains After Closing:
  • Only permanent accounts (Assets, Liabilities, Equity)
  • All temporary accounts have ZERO balances
  • Retained Earnings updated with current period's net income
  • Ready to start recording transactions for new period
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes:
  • Forgetting to close all revenue and expense accounts
  • Closing permanent accounts (DON'T close assets, liabilities, or capital!)
  • Recording closing entries in wrong order
  • Forgetting to close dividends/drawings account

üí∞ Income Statement: Basic Format

What is an Income Statement?

The Income Statement (also called Profit & Loss Statement or P&L) reports a company's financial performance over a specific period by showing revenues, expenses, and resulting profit or loss.

Net Income = Revenues - Expenses

Key Characteristics

  • Period of Time: Covers a specific period (month, quarter, year)
  • Performance Report: Shows profitability and operating efficiency
  • Basis: "For the Year Ended December 31, 2024"
  • Flow Statement: Dynamic‚Äîmeasures activity over time

Basic Single-Step Format

‚úÖ Income Statement Uses:
  • Assess profitability and operating efficiency
  • Compare performance across periods
  • Evaluate revenue and expense trends
  • Calculate profit margins and ratios
  • Make business decisions about pricing and costs

‚öñÔ∏è Balance Sheet: Basic Format

What is a Balance Sheet?

The Balance Sheet (also called Statement of Financial Position) reports a company's assets, liabilities, and owner's equity at a specific point in time.

Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity

Key Characteristics

  • Point in Time: Snapshot at a specific date
  • Position Report: Shows what the company owns and owes
  • Basis: "As of December 31, 2024"
  • Stock Statement: Static‚Äîmeasures position at moment in time
  • Always Balances: Both sides must equal

Balance Sheet Formats

Side-by-side presentation:

  • Assets on the LEFT

  • Traditional format
  • Emphasizes the equation

Report Format (Vertical)

Top-to-bottom presentation:

  • Assets listed FIRST
  • Liabilities & Equity listed BELOW
  • More common today
  • Easier to read

üìã Financial Statements

The Complete Set of Financial Statements

A complete set of financial statements includes four primary statements that work together to provide a comprehensive view of a company's financial health.

1. Income Statement üí∞

Purpose: Measures profitability

Period: For a period of time

Shows: Revenues, Expenses, Net Income

Answers: "Did we make a profit?"

2. Balance Sheet ‚öñÔ∏è

Purpose: Shows financial position

Period: At a point in time

Shows: Assets, Liabilities, Equity

Answers: "What do we own and owe?"

3. Statement of Owner's Equity üë§

Purpose: Shows changes in equity

Period: For a period of time

Shows: Beginning balance, investments, net income, withdrawals, ending balance

Answers: "How did equity change?"

4. Statement of Cash Flows üíµ

Purpose: Explains cash changes

Period: For a period of time

Shows: Operating, Investing, Financing activities

Answers: "Where did cash come from and go?"

Statement of Owner's Equity Example

ABC COMPANY
Statement of Owner's Equity
For the Year Ended December 31, 2024
Owner's Equity, January 1, 2024 $100,000
Add: Additional Investments 20,000
Add: Net Income for 2024 85,000
Subtotal $205,000
Less: Owner Withdrawals (48,000)
Owner's Equity, December 31, 2024 $157,000

How Financial Statements Connect

üîó The Relationship Between Statements:
  1. Income Statement calculates Net Income
  2. Net Income flows to Statement of Owner's Equity
  3. Ending Owner's Equity flows to Balance Sheet
  4. Cash on Balance Sheet ties to Statement of Cash Flows
üìä Flow of Information:

Income Statement: Net Income = $85,000
‚Üì
Statement of Owner's Equity: Add $85,000 to beginning equity
‚Üì
Balance Sheet: Owner's Equity section shows ending balance of $157,000

Preparation Order

‚úì Correct Sequence:
  1. Income Statement - First (determines net income)
  2. Statement of Owner's Equity - Second (uses net income)
  3. Balance Sheet - Third (uses ending equity balance)
  4. Statement of Cash Flows - Last (explains cash change on balance sheet)

üéØ Key Takeaway: Financial statements don't exist in isolation. They're interconnected reports that together tell the complete story of a business's financial health, performance, and cash position.

Understanding Which Accounts Go Where

Proper account classification is crucial for accurate financial reporting. Each account belongs to a specific financial statement.

Account Classification by Statement

üìä Balance Sheet Accounts (Permanent Accounts)

Assets:
  • Current: Cash, Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Prepaid Expenses, Marketable Securities, Notes Receivable (short-term)
  • Non-Current: Property/Plant/Equipment, Land, Buildings, Vehicles, Accumulated Depreciation (contra), Long-Term Investments, Intangible Assets, Goodwill
Liabilities:
  • Current: Accounts Payable, Accrued Expenses, Unearned Revenue, Current Portion of Long-Term Debt, Notes Payable (short-term), Salaries Payable
  • Non-Current: Long-Term Notes Payable, Bonds Payable, Mortgage Payable, Lease Obligations
Owner's Equity:
  • Common Stock, Preferred Stock, Retained Earnings, Additional Paid-in Capital, Treasury Stock (contra), Owner's Capital

üí∞ Income Statement Accounts (Temporary Accounts)

Revenues (Credits increase):
  • Sales Revenue, Service Revenue, Interest Revenue, Rental Revenue, Commission Revenue, Fees Earned, Dividend Revenue
Expenses (Debits increase):
  • Cost of Sales: Cost of Goods Sold
  • Operating Expenses: Salaries Expense, Rent Expense, Utilities Expense, Advertising Expense, Insurance Expense, Supplies Expense, Depreciation Expense, Bad Debt Expense
  • Other Expenses: Interest Expense, Loss on Sale of Assets

Quick Reference Chart

Account Type Financial Statement Normal Balance Examples
Assets Balance Sheet Debit Cash, Inventory, Equipment
Liabilities Balance Sheet Credit Accounts Payable, Notes Payable
Owner's Equity Balance Sheet Credit Capital, Retained Earnings
Revenues Income Statement Credit Sales, Service Revenue
Expenses Income Statement Debit Rent, Salaries, Utilities
Drawings/Dividends Statement of Owner's Equity Debit Owner's Drawings, Dividends

Special Cases and Contra Accounts

‚ö†Ô∏è Contra Accounts (Opposite Normal Balance):
  • Accumulated Depreciation: Contra-asset (Credit balance) - Balance Sheet
  • Allowance for Doubtful Accounts: Contra-asset (Credit balance) - Balance Sheet
  • Sales Returns & Allowances: Contra-revenue (Debit balance) - Income Statement
  • Treasury Stock: Contra-equity (Debit balance) - Balance Sheet
  • Discount on Bonds Payable: Contra-liability (Debit balance) - Balance Sheet

Common Classification Errors to Avoid

‚ùå Common Mistakes:

  • Putting expenses on balance sheet
  • Confusing prepaid expenses (asset) with expenses
  • Treating dividends as an expense
  • Misclassifying current vs. non-current items
  • Forgetting accumulated depreciation is contra-asset

‚úÖ Tips for Success:

  • Ask: "Is this a resource (asset), obligation (liability), or result of operations (revenue/expense)?"
  • Remember: Balance sheet = what you HAVE; Income statement = what you DID
  • Use the accounting equation as a guide
  • Check normal balances to verify classification
üéØ Master Rule: Permanent accounts (Assets, Liabilities, Equity) ‚Üí Balance Sheet. Temporary accounts (Revenues, Expenses) ‚Üí Income Statement. These temporary accounts are closed at period-end and their net effect updates owner's equity on the balance sheet.
üí° Final Thought: Proper account allocation ensures accurate financial statements, which are the foundation for sound business decisions, regulatory compliance, and stakeholder confidence. Master this skill, and you'll have a solid understanding of financial accounting fundamentals!
