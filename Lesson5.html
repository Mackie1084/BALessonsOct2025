📦 Inventory Systems

What is Inventory?

Inventory refers to goods held for sale in the ordinary course of business, including raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods.

Perpetual Inventory System

Real-time tracking of inventory

  • Continuous updates
  • Instant stock levels
  • Requires technology
  • More accurate
  • Higher cost

Periodic Inventory System

Physical count at intervals

  • Count at period end
  • Less frequent updates
  • Manual tracking
  • Less accurate
  • Lower cost

Modern Trend

Most businesses in Singapore now use perpetual systems with barcode scanning and inventory management software for real-time accuracy.

📊 Inventory Valuation Methods

1. FIFO (First-In, First-Out)

The first items purchased are the first ones sold. Oldest inventory costs matched against revenue.

Best for: Perishable goods, fashion items

2. Weighted Average Cost

Average cost of all units available for sale during the period.

Formula: Total Cost of Goods Available ÷ Total Units Available

Best for: Homogeneous products, commodities

3. Specific Identification

Each inventory item is tracked individually with its actual cost.

Best for: High-value unique items (cars, jewelry, art)

⚠️ Note: LIFO (Last-In, First-Out) is NOT permitted under Singapore Financial Reporting Standards (SFRS) and International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS).

How They Connect

When goods are sold on credit:

  • Inventory decreases (asset reduces)
  • Accounts Receivable increases (new asset created)
  • Cost of Goods Sold increases (expense recognized)
  • Sales Revenue increases (income recognized)

Example: Singapore Retailer

TechMart Pte Ltd sells a laptop:

• Inventory Cost: SGD $800
• Selling Price: SGD $1,200 + GST
• Customer: ABC Company (30-day credit terms)

Impact: Inventory ↓ $800 | A/R ↑ $1,308 | COGS ↑ $800 | Sales ↑ $1,200

✅ Three-Way Match Process

What is Three-Way Match?

A control procedure that verifies three documents match before processing payment to suppliers.

1️⃣ Purchase Order

What was ORDERED

  • Item description
  • Quantity
  • Price agreed
  • Terms

2️⃣ Receiving Report

What was RECEIVED

  • Items delivered
  • Quantity checked
  • Condition verified
  • Date received

3️⃣ Supplier Invoice

What is BILLED

  • Items charged
  • Quantity billed
  • Price charged
  • Payment terms

Why It Matters

✓ Prevents overpayment
✓ Ensures goods received match what was ordered
✓ Detects errors or fraud
✓ Controls inventory accuracy
✓ Verifies proper authorization

⚠️ Red Flags: Mismatches in quantity, price, or descriptions should be investigated before payment approval.

💵 Accounts Receivable (A/R)

What is Accounts Receivable?

Money owed to a business by customers for goods or services delivered on credit. It's a current asset on the balance sheet.

Importance of A/R Management

  • Improves cash flow
  • Reduces bad debts
  • Maintains customer relationships
  • Provides working capital
  • Indicates business health

Key A/R Metrics

Days Sales Outstanding (DSO):
Average collection period

A/R Turnover Ratio:
How quickly receivables convert to cash

Aging Analysis:
Breakdown by overdue period

Singapore Business Example

PrintPro Pte Ltd has SGD $150,000 in accounts receivable:

• Current (0-30 days): SGD $100,000
• 31-60 days overdue: SGD $30,000
• 61-90 days overdue: SGD $15,000
• Over 90 days: SGD $5,000 (high risk)

⚙️ A/R Processing Operations

3. Goods Delivered / Service Rendered

6. Payment Follow-up

8. Update A/R Ledger & Bank

💸 Bad Debts & Allowances

What are Bad Debts?

Accounts receivable that are unlikely to be collected. When a customer cannot or will not pay, the debt must be written off as an expense.

Direct Write-Off Method

Write off bad debt when it becomes uncollectible

Pros: Simple, actual losses
Cons: Violates matching principle

Allowance Method

Estimate uncollectible amounts in advance

Pros: Matches expenses with revenue
Cons: Based on estimates

Example: Writing Off Bad Debt

Scenario: GHI Trading (owing SGD $2,000 for over 90 days) has gone bankrupt. Write off the debt.

Journal Entry - Bad Debt Write-Off
Dr Bad Debts Expense SGD 2,000
Cr Accounts Receivable - GHI Trading SGD 2,000

This removes the uncollectible amount from A/R and records the loss.

Prevention Strategies

  • Conduct credit checks before extending credit
  • Set clear credit terms and policies
  • Send timely invoices and reminders
  • Offer early payment discounts
  • Use collection agencies for overdue accounts

💳 Accounts Payable (A/P)

What is Accounts Payable?

Money owed by a business to suppliers and creditors for goods or services received on credit. It's a current liability on the balance sheet.

Importance of A/P Management

  • Maintains supplier relationships
  • Optimizes cash flow
  • Takes advantage of discounts
  • Avoids late payment penalties
  • Preserves credit rating

Key A/P Metrics

Days Payable Outstanding (DPO):
Average time to pay suppliers

A/P Turnover Ratio:
How often A/P is paid off

Payment Terms:
Net 30, Net 60, 2/10 Net 30

Payment Terms Explained

Net 30: Full payment due in 30 days
2/10 Net 30: 2% discount if paid within 10 days, otherwise full payment due in 30 days
EOM (End of Month): Payment due at month end

📚 A/P Subsidiary Ledger

What is a Subsidiary Ledger?

A detailed ledger that tracks transactions with each individual supplier. The total of all subsidiary ledgers must equal the Accounts Payable control account in the general ledger.

Example: Bean Suppliers Ltd - Subsidiary Ledger

Date Description Invoice # Debit (Payments) Credit (Purchases) Balance
Jan 1, 2024 Opening Balance - - - 5,000.00
Jan 5, 2024 Coffee beans purchase INV-2401 - 3,270.00 8,270.00
Jan 12, 2024 Payment - Bank transfer - 5,000.00 - 3,270.00
Jan 15, 2024 Payment with discount - 3,270.00 - 0.00

Key Points:

  • Each supplier has their own subsidiary ledger
  • Shows complete transaction history
  • Helps track payment schedules
  • Identifies outstanding invoices
  • Sum of all subsidiary ledgers = A/P control account

⚖️ Accounts Receivable Reconciliation

What is A/R Reconciliation?

The process of verifying that the A/R control account in the general ledger matches the total of all individual customer accounts in the subsidiary ledger.

Why Reconcile A/R?

  • Ensures accuracy of financial records
  • Identifies posting errors or omissions
  • Detects potential fraud or theft
  • Provides confidence in financial statements
  • Facilitates audit process

A/R Reconciliation Template

A/R Control Account Balance (General Ledger) SGD 145,000
Subsidiary Ledger Totals:
ABC Company SGD 45,000
XYZ Corp SGD 32,000
DEF Enterprises SGD 28,000
GHI Trading SGD 25,000
Others (10 customers) SGD 15,000
Total Subsidiary Ledger SGD 145,000
Difference SGD 0 ✓

✓ Reconciliation Complete - Balances Match!

🔍 A/R Reconciliation Process

1. Print A/R Control Account from General Ledger
2. Print All Customer Subsidiary Ledgers
3. Sum All Customer Balances
4. Compare Total to Control Account
5. Investigate Any Differences
6. Make Correcting Entries if Needed
7. Document Reconciliation
8. Management Review & Sign-off

Common Causes of Discrepancies:

  • ❌ Invoice posted to wrong customer account
  • ❌ Payment received not recorded
  • ❌ Credit note not applied
  • ❌ Transposition error in amounts
  • ❌ Duplicate entry
  • ❌ Journal entry posted only to control account

Best Practices:

  • Reconcile monthly (minimum)
  • Weekly reconciliation for high-volume businesses
  • Independent person should perform reconciliation
  • Keep documentation for audit trail
  • Use accounting software with automatic reconciliation

⚖️ Accounts Payable Reconciliation

What is A/P Reconciliation?

The process of verifying that the A/P control account in the general ledger matches the total of all individual supplier accounts in the subsidiary ledger.

Why Reconcile A/P?

📄 Supplier Statement Reconciliation

What is Supplier Statement Reconciliation?

Comparing the supplier's statement of account with your A/P subsidiary ledger to ensure both parties have recorded the same transactions.

Your Records (A/P Ledger)

What YOU think you owe

  • Invoices received
  • Credit notes applied
  • Payments made
  • Your calculated balance

Supplier Statement

What SUPPLIER says you owe

  • Invoices issued
  • Credit notes issued
  • Payments received
  • Supplier's balance

Example: Reconciliation with Differences

Per Supplier Statement SGD 15,500
Reconciling Items:
Add: Invoice not yet in our books SGD 2,000
Less: Payment in transit (not yet received by supplier) (SGD 1,500)
Less: Credit note issued but not received (SGD 1,000)
Per Our A/P Ledger SGD 15,000

Actions Required:

  • Record the SGD $2,000 invoice in our books
  • Confirm supplier received our SGD $1,500 payment
  • Follow up on SGD $1,000 credit note
  • Adjust A/P ledger accordingly

📊 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)

Formula: (Accounts Receivable ÷ Total Credit Sales) × Number of Days

What it means: Average number of days to collect payment from customers

Good benchmark: 30-45 days for most industries

DSO Example

TechMart Pte Ltd has:

  • A/R Balance: SGD $145,000
  • Quarterly Credit Sales: SGD $900,000
  • Days in period: 90 days

DSO = (145,000 ÷ 900,000) × 90 = 14.5 days

✓ Excellent collection performance!

Days Payable Outstanding (DPO)

Formula: (Accounts Payable ÷ Cost of Goods Sold) × Number of Days

What it means: Average number of days to pay suppliers

Strategy: Balance between cash flow and supplier relationships

DPO Example

ElectroHub Pte Ltd has:

  • A/P Balance: SGD $89,500
  • Quarterly COGS: SGD $600,000
  • Days in period: 90 days

DPO = (89,500 ÷ 600,000) × 90 = 13.4 days

⚠️ Paying very quickly - may be missing discount opportunities or optimal cash flow timing

💼 Working Capital Management

The Cash Conversion Cycle

The time it takes to convert inventory and receivables into cash, minus payables period.

Cash Conversion Cycle

Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)
+
Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)
−
Days Payable Outstanding (DPO)
=
Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

Singapore Company Example

RetailPro Pte Ltd:

Days to sell inventory (DIO) 45 days
Days to collect from customers (DSO) 30 days
Days to pay suppliers (DPO) 40 days
Cash Conversion Cycle 35 days

Meaning: It takes 35 days from when cash is paid out to suppliers until cash is collected from customers.

Improving the Cash Conversion Cycle

  • Reduce DIO: Faster inventory turnover, better demand forecasting
  • Reduce DSO: Faster collections, stricter credit policies
  • Increase DPO: Negotiate longer payment terms (without damaging relationships)
  • Goal: Lower CCC = Better cash flow

⚠️ Common Issues & Solutions

Issue 1: Slow-Paying Customers

Problem: Customers consistently pay late, affecting cash flow

Solutions:

  • Implement early payment discounts (e.g., 2/10 Net 30)
  • Send reminder emails before due date
  • Call customers on the due date
  • Consider factoring receivables for immediate cash
  • Tighten credit terms for repeat offenders

Issue 2: Inventory Not Matching A/P Records

Problem: Physical inventory doesn't match what accounts payable shows as purchased

Solutions:

  • Implement proper receiving procedures with documentation
  • Conduct regular physical inventory counts
  • Use barcode/RFID scanning systems
  • Ensure three-way match is strictly followed
  • Investigate discrepancies immediately

Issue 3: Duplicate Payments to Suppliers

Problem: Same invoice paid twice due to poor controls

Solutions:

  • Stamp invoices "PAID" after processing
  • Use accounting software with duplicate detection
  • Reconcile A/P before each payment run
  • Require invoice number and date on payment authorization
  • Regular A/P subsidiary ledger review

🔒 Internal Controls for A/R and A/P

A/R Internal Controls

  • Credit approval before sales
  • Prenumbered invoices
  • Segregation of duties (sales vs collections)
  • Bank deposits match receipts
  • Regular aging report reviews
  • Write-off approvals documented
  • Customer statements reconciled

A/P Internal Controls

  • Purchase orders required
  • Three-way match before payment
  • Segregation of duties (ordering vs payment)
  • Authorized signatories for payments
  • Regular supplier statement reconciliation
  • Payment approval hierarchy
  • Prenumbered checks or payment batches

Fraud Prevention

Common fraud schemes:

  • 🚨 Fictitious vendors
  • 🚨 Kickbacks from suppliers
  • 🚨 Lapping (covering stolen receipts with subsequent collections)
  • 🚨 Invoice manipulation
  • 🚨 Ghost employees on payroll

Prevention: Strong internal controls, segregation of duties, regular audits, and surprise inspections
