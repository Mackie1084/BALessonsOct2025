1. Trial Balance - Periodic Inventory System

üìù Overview

In a periodic inventory system, inventory is counted at the end of each accounting period, and Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) is calculated using the formula.

COGS Formula:
Beginning Inventory + Purchases - Ending Inventory = Cost of Goods Sold

üîÑ Balance Day Adjustments

  • Closing Opening Inventory: Debit Income Summary, Credit Inventory
  • Recording Ending Inventory: Debit Inventory, Credit Income Summary
  • Accruals & Deferrals: Accrued expenses, prepaid expenses, accrued revenue, unearned revenue
  • Bad Debts: Estimate and record uncollectible accounts
  • Depreciation: Record depreciation expense for fixed assets

üìä Closing Journals (Periodic)

Step Account Debited Account Credited Purpose
1 Income Summary Revenue Accounts Close revenue accounts
2 Expense Accounts Income Summary Close expense accounts
3 Income Summary Opening Inventory Close opening inventory
4 Closing Inventory Income Summary Record closing inventory
5 Income Summary Capital/Retained Earnings Transfer net profit
6 Capital/Retained Earnings Drawings/Dividends Close drawings
üí° Key Point: In periodic inventory, purchases are recorded in a separate Purchases account, not directly to Inventory. The Inventory account is only updated at period end.

2. Trial Balance - Perpetual Inventory System

üìù Overview

In a perpetual inventory system, inventory records are updated continuously with each purchase and sale. COGS is calculated with each sale transaction.

Key Differences from Periodic System

  • Real-time tracking: Inventory balance updated with each transaction
  • COGS recorded immediately: When goods are sold, COGS is debited and Inventory is credited
  • No Purchases account: Purchases are debited directly to Inventory
  • Better inventory control: Provides current inventory levels at any time

üîÑ Balance Day Adjustments

  • Inventory Shrinkage: Adjust for physical count vs. book value differences
  • Accruals & Deferrals: Same as periodic system
  • Bad Debts: Estimate and record uncollectible accounts
  • Depreciation: Record depreciation expense for fixed assets

üìä Closing Journals (Perpetual)

Step Account Debited Account Credited Purpose
1 Revenue Accounts Income Summary Close revenue accounts
2 Income Summary Expense Accounts (including COGS) Close all expense accounts
3 Income Summary Capital/Retained Earnings Transfer net profit (or reverse for loss)
4 Capital/Retained Earnings Drawings/Dividends Close drawings or dividends
üìå Example Entry for Sale:
Dr. Cash/Accounts Receivable
    Cr. Sales Revenue
Dr. Cost of Goods Sold
    Cr. Inventory
üí° Key Point: No inventory adjustment entries needed at year-end in perpetual system (except for shrinkage), as inventory is already at correct balance.

3. Financial Statements from 10-Column Worksheets

üìã What is a 10-Column Worksheet?

A 10-column worksheet is an accounting tool used to organize trial balance data and adjustments to prepare financial statements efficiently.

Structure of 10-Column Worksheet

Account Name Trial Balance Adjustments Adjusted Trial Balance Income Statement Balance Sheet
Dr Cr Dr Cr Dr Cr Dr Cr Dr Cr

üìù Steps to Complete the Worksheet

  • Step 1: Enter unadjusted trial balance in first two columns
  • Step 2: Record adjusting entries in adjustment columns
  • Step 3: Calculate adjusted trial balance (combine TB + Adjustments)
  • Step 4: Extend revenue and expense accounts to Income Statement columns
  • Step 5: Extend asset, liability, and equity accounts to Balance Sheet columns
  • Step 6: Calculate net income/loss and complete the worksheet

üéØ Purpose and Benefits

  • Organizes all accounting data in one place
  • Helps identify errors before preparing financial statements
  • Simplifies preparation of Income Statement and Balance Sheet
  • Provides clear audit trail of adjustments
  • Saves time in financial statement preparation
üí° Important: The worksheet is an internal document and is NOT part of the official accounting records. It's a tool for organizing information.

4. Preparation of Financial Statements

üìä Main Financial Statements

1. Income Statement (Statement of Comprehensive Income)

Purpose: Shows financial performance over a period

Formula: Revenue - Expenses = Net Income/Loss

Components:

  • Revenue (Sales, Service Revenue)
  • Cost of Goods Sold
  • Gross Profit (Revenue - COGS)
  • Operating Expenses
  • Operating Income
  • Other Income and Expenses
  • Net Income/Loss

2. Statement of Changes in Equity

Purpose: Shows changes in owner's equity during the period

Components:

  • Beginning Capital/Retained Earnings
  • Add: Net Income (or subtract Net Loss)
  • Add: Additional Capital Contributions
  • Less: Drawings/Dividends
  • Ending Capital/Retained Earnings

3. Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position)

Purpose: Shows financial position at a specific date

Fundamental Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

Components:

  • Assets: Current Assets, Non-Current Assets
  • Liabilities: Current Liabilities, Non-Current Liabilities
  • Equity: Capital, Retained Earnings, Reserves

üìã Order of Preparation

  1. Income Statement - Prepare first to determine net income/loss
  2. Statement of Changes in Equity - Uses net income from IS
  3. Balance Sheet - Uses ending equity from SOCE
üí° Critical Rule: Financial statements must be prepared in order because each statement provides information needed for the next one.

5. Correction of Errors

üîç Types of Errors

Error Type Description Effect on Trial Balance
Error of Omission Transaction completely omitted from books Will still balance
Error of Commission Entry made to wrong account of same type Will still balance
Error of Principle Entry made to wrong account type Will still balance
Compensating Error Two or more errors offset each other Will still balance
Error of Original Entry Wrong amount recorded in both accounts Will still balance
Reversal of Entry Debit and credit reversed Will still balance
Single-sided Entry Only one side of entry recorded Will NOT balance
Unequal Entry Different amounts debited and credited Will NOT balance

üîß Correction Methods

Method 1: Journal Entry Method

Create a correcting journal entry that reverses the error and records the correct entry.

Example: Rent expense of $500 was debited to Repairs

Correction: Dr. Rent Expense $500 / Cr. Repairs Expense $500

Method 2: Suspense Account (for unbalanced TB)

When trial balance doesn't balance, difference goes to Suspense Account.

Steps:

  • Identify the error
  • Make correcting entry
  • Clear Suspense Account balance

üìù Steps to Correct Errors

  1. Identify the error and its nature
  2. Determine the correct entry that should have been made
  3. Compare with the incorrect entry actually made
  4. Create correcting journal entry to fix the difference
  5. Post the correction to ledger accounts
üí° Pro Tip: Always show your working when correcting errors. Document what was wrong, what should be, and how you're fixing it.

6. Depreciation Calculation Methods

üí∞ What is Depreciation?

Depreciation is the systematic allocation of the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life. It represents the consumption of economic benefits.

üìä Main Depreciation Methods

1. Straight-Line Method

Formula: (Cost - Salvage Value) / Useful Life

Annual Depreciation = (Cost - Residual Value) / Number of Years

Features:
  • Equal depreciation expense each year
  • Most commonly used method
  • Simple to calculate and understand

2. Reducing Balance Method (Diminishing Balance)

Formula: Book Value √ó Depreciation Rate

Annual Depreciation = (Cost - Accumulated Depreciation) √ó Rate %

Features:
  • Higher depreciation in early years
  • Depreciation decreases each year
  • Suitable for assets that lose value quickly

3. Units of Production Method

Formula: (Cost - Salvage Value) / Total Units √ó Units Produced

Depreciation per Unit = (Cost - Residual Value) / Total Expected Units
Annual Depreciation = Depreciation per Unit √ó Units Produced in Year

Features:
  • Based on actual usage or production
  • Variable depreciation expense
  • Suitable for manufacturing equipment

üìù Recording Depreciation

Journal Entry:

Dr. Depreciation Expense
    Cr. Accumulated Depreciation

Example: Annual depreciation of $5,000
Dr. Depreciation Expense $5,000
    Cr. Accumulated Depreciation $5,000
üí° Important Concepts:
  • Cost: Original purchase price plus installation costs
  • Residual/Salvage Value: Expected value at end of useful life
  • Useful Life: Period over which asset is expected to be used
  • Book Value: Cost - Accumulated Depreciation

7. De-recognition or Disposal of Depreciable Assets

üóëÔ∏è What is Asset Disposal?

Asset disposal occurs when a company removes an asset from its books because it has been sold, traded, scrapped, or is no longer in use.

üìã Steps for Asset Disposal

  1. Update Depreciation: Record depreciation up to date of disposal
  2. Remove Asset Cost: Credit the asset account for original cost
  3. Remove Accumulated Depreciation: Debit accumulated depreciation
  4. Record Proceeds: Debit cash or accounts receivable
  5. Calculate Gain/Loss: Balance to gain or loss on disposal

üíµ Calculating Gain or Loss on Disposal

Book Value = Cost - Accumulated Depreciation

Gain/Loss = Proceeds from Sale - Book Value

‚Ä¢ If Proceeds > Book Value = GAIN
‚Ä¢ If Proceeds < Book Value = LOSS
‚Ä¢ If Proceeds = Book Value = NO GAIN/LOSS

üìù Journal Entries for Disposal

Scenario 1: Sale at Gain

Example: Equipment cost $50,000, Accumulated Depreciation $35,000, Sold for $20,000

Book Value = $50,000 - $35,000 = $15,000

Gain = $20,000 - $15,000 = $5,000


Journal Entry:
Dr. Cash $20,000
Dr. Accumulated Depreciation $35,000
    Cr. Equipment $50,000
    Cr. Gain on Disposal $5,000

Scenario 2: Sale at Loss

Example: Vehicle cost $30,000, Accumulated Depreciation $20,000, Sold for $7,000

Book Value = $30,000 - $20,000 = $10,000

Loss = $7,000 - $10,000 = -$3,000 (Loss)


Journal Entry:
Dr. Cash $7,000
Dr. Accumulated Depreciation $20,000
Dr. Loss on Disposal $3,000
    Cr. Vehicle $30,000

Scenario 3: Asset Scrapped (No Proceeds)

Example: Machine cost $40,000, Accumulated Depreciation $38,000, Scrapped

Book Value = $40,000 - $38,000 = $2,000

Loss = $0 - $2,000 = -$2,000 (Loss)


Journal Entry:
Dr. Accumulated Depreciation $38,000
Dr. Loss on Disposal $2,000
    Cr. Machine $40,000

üîÑ Disposal During the Year

Important: If disposal occurs during the accounting period (not at year-end), you must:
  1. Calculate and record depreciation from beginning of year to disposal date
  2. Update Accumulated Depreciation account
  3. Then proceed with disposal entries

Pro-rata Depreciation: Annual Depreciation √ó (Months Used / 12)

üíº Impact on Financial Statements

  • Income Statement: Gain/Loss on disposal appears as other income/expense
  • Balance Sheet: Asset and accumulated depreciation removed
  • Cash Flow Statement: Proceeds from disposal in investing activities
üí° Key Points to Remember:
  • Always update depreciation before disposal
  • Both asset cost AND accumulated depreciation must be removed
  • Gain is credited (increases income), Loss is debited (decreases income)
  • Cash or receivable is debited for proceeds received
