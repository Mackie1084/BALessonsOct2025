1. The Accounting Equation

The accounting equation is the foundation of double-entry bookkeeping and forms the basis of the balance sheet.

Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity

Why It Matters

This equation must always balance. Every transaction affects at least two accounts, maintaining equilibrium in the financial records.

Interactive Calculator

Example:

If a company has $100,000 in assets and $40,000 in liabilities, then:

Owner's Equity = $100,000 - $40,000 = $60,000

2. Assets

Assets are resources owned by a business that have economic value and can provide future benefits.

Key Characteristics

  • Controlled by the business
  • Result from past transactions
  • Expected to provide future economic benefits

Common Examples

  • Cash: Money in bank accounts and on hand
  • Accounts Receivable: Money owed by customers
  • Inventory: Goods available for sale
  • Equipment: Machinery and tools
  • Buildings: Real estate property
  • Vehicles: Company transportation

Real-World Example:

A retail store's assets include its cash register ($500), inventory of products ($25,000), store fixtures ($10,000), and the building it owns ($200,000).

3. Liabilities

Liabilities are obligations or debts that a business owes to external parties.

Key Characteristics

  • Present obligations of the business
  • Result from past transactions
  • Require future payment or service

Common Examples

  • Accounts Payable: Money owed to suppliers
  • Loans Payable: Bank loans and borrowed funds
  • Wages Payable: Salaries owed to employees
  • Taxes Payable: Tax obligations to government
  • Mortgages: Long-term property loans
  • Bonds Payable: Corporate debt securities

Real-World Example:

A manufacturing company owes $15,000 to suppliers (accounts payable), has a $100,000 bank loan, and must pay $5,000 in employee wages next week.

4. Owner's Equity

Owner's equity represents the owner's claim to the business assets after all liabilities have been paid.

Owner's Equity = Assets - Liabilities

Components of Owner's Equity

  • Capital Contributions: Money invested by owners
  • Retained Earnings: Accumulated profits kept in business
  • Drawings: Money withdrawn by owners (reduces equity)
  • Additional Paid-in Capital: Excess over par value (corporations)

Understanding Owner's Equity:

Think of it as the "net worth" of the business. If you sold all assets and paid off all debts, what remains is owner's equity.

Example: A business with $500,000 in assets and $200,000 in liabilities has owner's equity of $300,000.

5. Current and Non-Current Assets

Current Assets

Assets expected to be converted to cash or used within one year or operating cycle.

  • Cash and cash equivalents
  • Accounts receivable
  • Inventory
  • Prepaid expenses
  • Short-term investments

Non-Current Assets (Fixed Assets)

Assets that provide value over more than one year.

  • Property, Plant & Equipment (PP&E)
  • Long-term investments
  • Intangible assets (patents, trademarks)
  • Goodwill
  • Land and buildings

Why the Distinction Matters:

Current assets indicate a company's liquidity and ability to meet short-term obligations. Non-current assets show long-term value and investment in growth.

6. Current and Non-Current Liabilities

Current Liabilities

Obligations due within one year or operating cycle.

  • Accounts payable
  • Short-term loans
  • Wages payable
  • Taxes payable
  • Interest payable
  • Current portion of long-term debt

Non-Current Liabilities (Long-Term)

Obligations not due within the next year.

  • Long-term loans
  • Mortgages payable
  • Bonds payable
  • Deferred tax liabilities
  • Long-term lease obligations

Financial Health Indicator:

The ratio of current assets to current liabilities (current ratio) indicates a company's ability to pay short-term debts. A ratio above 1.0 is generally healthy.

7. Owner's Equity Classification

Unlike assets and liabilities, owner's equity is typically not divided into current and non-current categories. However, it has important components:

Permanent Equity Components

  • Share Capital / Capital Stock: Initial and additional investments
  • Retained Earnings: Accumulated profits over time
  • Reserves: Amounts set aside for specific purposes
  • Treasury Stock: Company's own shares bought back (reduces equity)

Changes in Owner's Equity

  • Increases: Owner investments, net income
  • Decreases: Owner withdrawals, net losses, dividends

Note:

While equity doesn't have a maturity date like liabilities, some equity instruments (like redeemable preferred shares) may have specific redemption dates and could be classified separately.

9. The Expanded Accounting Equation

The expanded equation breaks down owner's equity to show all changes that affect it.

Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Capital + Revenues - Expenses - Drawings

Understanding Each Component

  • Assets: What the business owns
  • Liabilities: What the business owes
  • Owner's Capital: Initial and additional investments
  • Revenues: Income earned from operations (increases equity)
  • Expenses: Costs of doing business (decreases equity)
  • Drawings: Owner withdrawals (decreases equity)

How It Works:

Starting Position:

Assets: $100,000 = Liabilities: $40,000 + Capital: $60,000


After Operations:

+ Revenues: $50,000 (sales income)

- Expenses: $30,000 (costs)

- Drawings: $5,000 (owner withdrawal)


New Equity = $60,000 + $50,000 - $30,000 - $5,000 = $75,000

Why Use the Expanded Equation?

It shows how day-to-day business activities (revenues and expenses) and owner transactions (capital and drawings) affect the financial position over time.
