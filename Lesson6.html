1. Process Special Transactions

Special transactions are non-routine business events that require specific accounting treatment beyond standard sales, purchases, and cash transactions.

Why Special Transactions Matter

  • They affect the financial position in unique ways
  • Require specific journal entries and documentation
  • Impact both the balance sheet and income statement
  • Must be handled carefully to maintain accurate records

Categories of Special Transactions:

Owner-Related

  • Additional capital contributions
  • Drawings (cash and goods)

Receivables Management

  • Interest on overdue accounts
  • Dishonoured cheques
  • Bad debts write-offs and recoveries

Bills of Exchange

  • Bills receivable (accepting and receiving payment)
  • Bills payable (accepting and making payment)

Key Principle:

Each special transaction must be recorded using the appropriate journal and posted to the correct ledger accounts to maintain the accounting equation balance.

2. Introduction of Additional Capital

When an owner invests more money or assets into the business, it increases both the business assets and owner's equity.

Types of Capital Contributions:

Cash Capital

Owner deposits money into the business bank account

Asset Capital

Owner contributes equipment, vehicles, or other assets

Journal Entry for Cash Capital:

Example: Owner invests $10,000 cash on March 15

Date Account Debit Credit
Mar 15 Bank $10,000
Capital $10,000
Additional capital invested by owner

Journal Entry for Asset Capital:

Example: Owner contributes equipment worth $15,000

Date Account Debit Credit
Mar 20 Equipment $15,000
Capital $15,000
Equipment contributed by owner

Effect on Accounting Equation:

Assets ↑ = Liabilities + Owner's Equity ↑

3. Drawings of Cash and Goods

Drawings occur when the owner takes money or goods from the business for personal use. This reduces both assets and owner's equity.

Cash Drawings:

Example: Owner withdraws $2,000 cash on April 10

Date Account Debit Credit
Apr 10 Drawings $2,000
Bank $2,000
Cash withdrawn by owner for personal use

Drawings of Goods:

Example: Owner takes goods worth $500 for personal use

Date Account Debit Credit
Apr 15 Drawings $500
Purchases $500
Goods taken by owner for personal use

⚠️ Important Note:

Drawings reduce owner's equity! They are not expenses of the business but personal withdrawals by the owner.

Effect on Accounting Equation:

Assets ↓ = Liabilities + Owner's Equity ↓

4. Interest Receivable and Payable on Overdue Accounts

When customers pay late or suppliers allow extended payment terms, interest may be charged or earned on overdue amounts.

Interest Receivable (on Overdue Debtors):

Example: Customer ABC owes $5,000 for 60 days overdue. Interest charged at 10% p.a.

Interest = $5,000 × 10% × (60/365) = $82.19
Date Account Debit Credit
May 15 Accounts Receivable - ABC $82.19
Interest Income $82.19
Interest charged on overdue account

Interest Payable (on Overdue Creditors):

Example: We owe Supplier XYZ $8,000 for 45 days overdue. Interest charged at 12% p.a.

Interest = $8,000 × 12% × (45/365) = $118.36
Date Account Debit Credit
May 20 Interest Expense $118.36
Accounts Payable - XYZ $118.36
Interest charged by supplier on overdue account

5. Dishonour of a Cheque

A cheque is dishonoured when the bank refuses to pay it, usually due to insufficient funds in the drawer's account.

Step 1: Customer pays with cheque - recorded as cash receipt
Step 2: Bank dishonours cheque - money not received
Step 3: Reverse the original entry and reinstate debtor

Original Entry (When Cheque Received):

Customer Smith pays $3,000 by cheque

Date Account Debit Credit
Jun 5 Bank $3,000
Accounts Receivable - Smith $3,000

Reversal Entry (When Cheque Dishonoured):

Smith's cheque is dishonoured (plus $25 bank fee)

Date Account Debit Credit
Jun 10 Accounts Receivable - Smith $3,025
Bank $3,025
Cheque dishonoured - original amount plus bank charges

⚠️ Important:

The debtor now owes the original amount PLUS any bank charges. The business must collect this full amount from the customer.

6. Bad Debts Write-Offs

A bad debt occurs when a customer is unable or unwilling to pay, and the business determines the amount is uncollectible.

When to Write Off Bad Debts:

  • Customer has declared bankruptcy
  • Customer cannot be located
  • Legal action has been exhausted
  • Cost of collection exceeds the amount owed

Journal Entry for Bad Debt Write-Off:

Example: Customer Jones owes $1,500 but has gone bankrupt

Date Account Debit Credit
Jul 15 Bad Debts Expense $1,500
Accounts Receivable - Jones $1,500
Write-off of uncollectible debt

Effect on Financial Statements:

Income Statement

Bad Debts Expense increases ↑

This reduces Net Profit

Balance Sheet

Accounts Receivable decreases ↓

Assets are reduced

ACCOUNTS RECEIVABLE - JONES
Debit Side
Jun 1 Sales $1,500
Credit Side
Jul 15 Bad Debts $1,500
Account Balance: $0 (Closed)

⚠️ Remember:

Writing off a bad debt is NOT the same as giving up. The business should still attempt collection, and if successful, it becomes a bad debt recovered.

7. Bad Debts Recovered

Sometimes, after writing off a debt as bad, the customer pays all or part of the amount. This is called a bad debt recovery.

1. Original Sale
Customer owes money
→
2. Write-Off
Deemed uncollectible
→
3. Recovery
Payment received!

Two-Step Process for Recovery:

Step 1: Reinstate the Debtor Account

Example: Jones (previously written off) now pays $1,500

Date Account Debit Credit
Aug 20 Accounts Receivable - Jones $1,500
Bad Debts Recovered $1,500
Reinstatement of previously written-off debt

Step 2: Record the Cash Receipt

Date Account Debit Credit
Aug 20 Bank $1,500
Accounts Receivable - Jones $1,500
Payment received from debtor

✅ Accounting Treatment:

Bad Debts Recovered is recorded as income/revenue on the Income Statement, increasing profit for the current period.

Alternative Method (One Entry):

Some businesses record recovery directly:

Bank $1,500
Bad Debts Recovered $1,500

8. Bills Payable - Accepted and Met

A bill payable (or promissory note) is a written promise to pay a specific amount on a future date. It's issued by the business (debtor) to a creditor.

Key Features of Bills Payable:

  • Drawer: The creditor who will receive payment
  • Drawee: The business making the promise to pay
  • Maturity Date: When payment is due
  • Amount: Fixed sum to be paid

When Bill Payable is Accepted:

Example: We accept a 60-day bill for $8,000 to Supplier ABC on Sept 1

Date Account Debit Credit
Sep 1 Accounts Payable - ABC $8,000
Bills Payable $8,000
Acceptance of 60-day bill payable to ABC

When Bill Payable is Met (Paid):

Example: Pay the bill on maturity date (Nov 1)

Date Account Debit Credit
Nov 1 Bills Payable $8,000
Bank $8,000
Payment of bill on maturity
BILLS PAYABLE ACCOUNT
Debit Side (Payment)
Nov 1 Bank $8,000
Credit Side (Acceptance)
Sep 1 Accounts Payable $8,000

Benefits of Bills Payable:

  • Provides formal payment arrangement
  • May reduce immediate creditor pressure
  • Can be negotiated or discounted
  • Legal document for both parties

9. Bills Receivable - Accepted and Met

A bill receivable is a written promise from a customer to pay a specific amount on a future date. The business is the creditor holding the bill.

Key Features of Bills Receivable:

  • Drawer: The business (creditor) receiving payment
  • Drawee: The customer promising to pay
  • Maturity Date: When payment will be received
  • Amount: Fixed sum to be received

When Bill Receivable is Accepted:

Example: Customer XYZ accepts a 90-day bill for $12,000 on Oct 1

Date Account Debit Credit
Oct 1 Bills Receivable $12,000
Accounts Receivable - XYZ $12,000
90-day bill accepted by customer XYZ

When Bill Receivable is Met (Received):

Example: Receive payment on maturity date (Dec 30)

Date Account Debit Credit
Dec 30 Bank $12,000
Bills Receivable $12,000
Receipt of payment on maturity
BILLS RECEIVABLE ACCOUNT
Debit Side (Acceptance)
Oct 1 Accounts Receivable $12,000
Credit Side (Receipt)
Dec 30 Bank $12,000

✅ Benefits of Bills Receivable:

  • Legally enforceable promise to pay
  • Can be discounted at bank for immediate cash
  • Reduces risk of non-payment
  • Provides certainty on payment date

10. Bills Receivable vs Bills Payable

Bills Receivable

  • We are: The creditor
  • Customer owes us: Money
  • It is: An asset
  • On Balance Sheet: Current Asset
  • When accepted: Debit Bills Receivable
  • When met: Credit Bills Receivable

Bills Payable

  • We are: The debtor
  • We owe supplier: Money
  • It is: A liability
  • On Balance Sheet: Current Liability
  • When accepted: Credit Bills Payable
  • When met: Debit Bills Payable

Complete Lifecycle Comparison:

CR Bills Payable
During Term Shown as Current Asset Shown as Current Liability

Matching Transaction Example:

Scenario: Company A sells goods to Company B for $10,000. Company B accepts a 60-day bill.


Company A's Books (Seller):

DR Bills Receivable $10,000
CR Accounts Receivable - Company B $10,000


Company B's Books (Buyer):

CR Bills Payable $10,000

12. Summary: Special Transactions

Key Concepts Review

Transaction Type Effect
Additional Capital ↑ Assets, ↑ Equity
Drawings ↓ Assets, ↓ Equity
Interest Receivable ↑ Assets, ↑ Income
Dishonoured Cheque ↑ Receivables, ↓ Bank
Bad Debts Write-Off ↓ Assets, ↑ Expense
Bad Debts Recovered ↑ Bank, ↑ Income
Bills Receivable Asset (Promise from customer)
Bills Payable Liability (Promise to supplier)

Important Principles:

1. Documentation

Every special transaction requires proper source documents and clear narration in journal entries.

2. Account Classification

Correctly identify whether accounts are assets, liabilities, equity, income, or expenses.

3. Timing

Record transactions in the correct accounting period to match revenues with expenses.

4. Double Entry

Always maintain the accounting equation: every debit must have a corresponding credit.

✅ Best Practices:

  • Review customer accounts regularly to identify potential bad debts early
  • Calculate interest on overdue accounts according to business policy
  • Follow up promptly on dishonoured cheques
  • Maintain clear records of all bills receivable and payable
  • Keep owner drawings separate from business expenses
  • Document all special transactions with appropriate narrations

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Confusing bills receivable (asset) with bills payable (liability)
  • Forgetting to add bank charges to dishonoured cheque amount
  • Not reversing the original entry when a cheque is dishonoured
  • Recording drawings as business expenses
  • Forgetting to reinstate debtor account when bad debt is recovered
