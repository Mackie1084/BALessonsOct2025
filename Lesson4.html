1. Specialized Journals

Specialized journals are books of original entry designed to record specific types of transactions efficiently.

Why Use Specialized Journals?

  • Saves time by grouping similar transactions
  • Reduces errors through standardization
  • Allows division of labor in accounting departments
  • Simplifies posting to ledger accounts

Types of Specialized Journals:

üìó Sales Journal (SJ)

Records all credit sales of goods

üìï Purchases Journal (PJ)

Records all credit purchases of goods

üìò Cash Receipts Journal (CRJ)

Records all cash received

üìô Cash Payments Journal (CPJ)

Records all cash paid out

üìî General Journal (GJ)

Records transactions that don't fit in specialized journals (adjustments, corrections, opening entries)

Key Principle:

Each transaction is recorded ONCE in the appropriate journal, then posted to the relevant ledger accounts.

5. Cash Receipts Journal (CRJ)

Records all cash received by the business from any source.

Standard CRJ Format:

Date Details Doc Fol Bank Discount Allowed Sales Accounts Receivable Other
Jan 5 ABC Co R01 A1 970 30 - 1,000 -
Jan 8 Cash Sales R02 2,500 - 2,500 - -
Jan 12 Rent Received R03 1,500 - - - 1,500
Totals 4,970 30 2,500 1,000 1,500

Key Columns Explained:

  • Bank: Total cash deposited (debit)
  • Discount Allowed: Cash discounts given to customers (debit)
  • Sales: Cash sales revenue (credit)
  • Accounts Receivable: Collections from credit customers (credit)
  • Other: Miscellaneous receipts like rent, interest (credit)

Balancing Check:

Bank + Discount Allowed = Sales + Accounts Receivable + Other

6. Cash Book

The Cash Book combines the Cash Receipts Journal and Cash Payments Journal into one book. It serves as both a journal and a ledger account for cash/bank.

Two-Column Cash Book Example:

RECEIPTS (Dr) PAYMENTS (Cr)
Date Details Fol Amount Date Details Fol Amount
Jan 1 Balance b/d 5,000 Jan 3 Rent Paid 2,000
Jan 5 Cash Sales 3,000 Jan 7 Supplier Payment 1,500
Jan 15 Customer Payment A1 2,500 Jan 10 Salaries 4,000
Total Receipts 10,500 Total Payments 7,500
Jan 31 Balance c/d 3,000

Types of Cash Books:

  • Single Column: Records only bank transactions
  • Two Column: Separate columns for cash and bank
  • Three Column: Includes discount columns
  • Petty Cash Book: For small daily expenses

Dual Purpose:

The Cash Book is unique - it's both a journal of original entry and a ledger account for Bank/Cash.

7. Cash Payments Journal (CPJ)

Records all cash payments made by the business for any purpose.

Standard CPJ Format:

Date Details Chq No. Fol Bank Discount Received Accounts Payable Purchases Other
Jan 4 Global Suppliers 001 G2 1,950 50 2,000 - -
Jan 7 Cash Purchases 002 3,000 - - 3,000 -
Jan 10 Rent Expense 003 2,500 - - - 2,500
Jan 15 Salaries 004 5,000 - - - 5,000
Totals 12,450 50 2,000 3,000 7,500

Key Columns Explained:

  • Bank: Total cash paid out (credit)
  • Discount Received: Cash discounts from suppliers (credit)
  • Accounts Payable: Payments to credit suppliers (debit)
  • Purchases: Cash purchases of inventory (debit)
  • Other: Operating expenses like rent, salaries, utilities (debit)

Balancing Check:

Accounts Payable + Purchases + Other = Bank + Discount Received

8. Transaction Reviews

Regular review of journal entries ensures accuracy and completeness of financial records.

Transaction Review Process

1. Verify Source Document
2. Check Correct Journal Used
3. Confirm Amounts Accurate
4. Review Account Classifications
5. Ensure Complete Details
6. Check Cross-Referencing (Folio)

Common Errors to Check:

  • Wrong journal used
  • Incorrect amounts
  • Missing source documents
  • Transposition errors
  • Duplicate entries
  • Wrong account classification

Review Frequency:

  • Daily: Individual entries
  • Weekly: Journal totals
  • Monthly: Complete reconciliation
  • Quarterly: Comprehensive audit

Example Review Case:

Transaction: Received $950 from customer who owed $1,000

Check:

  • ‚úì Recorded in CRJ (correct journal)
  • ‚úì Bank: $950 (debit)
  • ‚úì Discount Allowed: $50 (debit)
  • ‚úì Accounts Receivable: $1,000 (credit)
  • ‚úì Source document: Receipt #456

Posting Rules:

  • Cross-reference: Always include journal reference (CRJ, CPJ, SJ, PJ)

10. Creating Accounting Reports & Statements

Journals and ledgers provide the data needed to create financial reports and statements.

Reporting Process Flow

Source Documents
Record in Journals
Post to Ledgers
Prepare Trial Balance
Create Financial Statements

Key Reports Generated:

1. Trial Balance

Lists all ledger account balances to verify debits equal credits

Account Debit Credit
Bank 15,000 -
Accounts Receivable 8,000 -
Accounts Payable - 5,000
Sales - 18,000
Totals 23,000 23,000

2. Income Statement

Shows profitability over a period

  • Revenue (from Sales Journal)
  • Cost of Goods Sold
  • Expenses (from CPJ)
  • Net Profit/Loss

3. Balance Sheet

Shows financial position at a date

  • Assets (from ledgers)
  • Liabilities (from ledgers)
  • Owner's Equity

4. Cash Flow Statement

Shows cash movements from CRJ and CPJ

  • Operating Activities: Cash from business operations
  • Investing Activities: Cash for assets
  • Financing Activities: Cash from/to owners and lenders
